\chapter*{Conclusions}
\label{chapter:conclusion}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{\textsc{Conclusions}}
\addtocontents{toc}{\protect\bigskip}

In this thesis, we have investigated the problem of control of partially specified Boolean networks that was not explored prior to starting this work. We mapped the state of the art in this area and made an exhaustive overview of the existing methods for control of asynchronous Boolean networks. Based on our investigation, we have proposed a novel problem formulation that captures the challenges of controlling partially specified Boolean networks. 

We have developed a suite of algorithms for solving this problem that are based on the previous methods for source-target control with modifications to handle the uncertainty in the model. We have seen that temporary and permanent perturbations are outperforming simpler one-shot interventions in terms of the size of the control sets and the robustness of the control strategy.

While exploring the source-target control, we noticed, that a specification of an attractor as a target state is not suitable for the control of biological systems, since partially specified models are prone to attractor shift, where different model interpretations have slightly different attractors. Therefore, we proposed a novel control problem formulation called phenotype control, where the target is defined as a set of states that share a common property, such as the activation of a specific gene or the presence of a specific phenotype. We have developed algorithms for solving this problem and demonstrated their applicability through case studies on real-world models.

We have implemented both source-target and phenotype control methods in the AEON tool. We provided a convenient Python interface for them and an un iteractive web-based user interface for the phenotype control.

Moreover, we explored the utilization of control-based method refinement as a means of guiding the refinement of partially specified Boolean networks. We have shown that this approach can be effective in identifying the most informative experiments to perform in order to refine the model and improve its alignment with real model behavior. We have also demonstrated the applicability of this approach through case studies on real-world models.

There are several directions for future research in this area. One promising avenue is to explore the combination of control-based method refinement with model checking to further enhance the efficiency and scalability of control methods for \PBN{s}. Additionally, it would be interesting to other types of control problems, such as phenotype control with temporary perturbations.
